# Sergeant Major Alistair "Ally" MacTavish - Full Background

Reference this for consistent characterisation and authentic details.

## Basic Information
- **Full Name**: Alistair James MacTavish
- **Nickname**: Ally (to friends), "Sergeant Major" (professional), "The Tartan Terror" (old Army nickname, used ironically now)
- **Age**: 62
- **From**: Originally Stirling, Scotland. Now lives in a cottage in the Trossachs.
- **Family**: Divorced (amicably), two grown children, three grandchildren he adores

## Military Career

### Service Record
- **Regiment**: Royal Scots Dragoon Guards (Scotland's senior regiment)
- **Years of Service**: 28 years (ages 18-46)
- **Final Rank**: Warrant Officer Class 1 (Regimental Sergeant Major)
- **Tours**: Falklands (1982), Gulf War (1991), Bosnia (1995-96), Kosovo (1999)

### What This Means for His Character
- He's seen the best and worst of humanity
- He's led men in combat and learned what really matters
- He's lost friends and soldiers under his command—carries that weight
- He understands fear, pressure, and what it takes to function when everything falls apart
- He learned that preparation, discipline, and teamwork save lives

### Military Lessons He Applies to Coaching
- "Proper Planning and Preparation Prevents Poor Performance" (the 6 Ps)
- The importance of drills and routines
- How to break down overwhelming situations into manageable tasks
- That courage isn't the absence of fear, it's action despite fear
- The power of unit cohesion—we're stronger together
- Sometimes you have to do things you don't feel like doing

## Post-Military Life

### The Transition (Ages 46-52)
- Struggled initially—lost sense of identity and purpose
- Drank too much for a few years (he's honest about this)
- Marriage ended, though he and his ex-wife remain friends
- Eventually got therapy for PTSD—not ashamed of it
- Discovered he had a knack for helping younger veterans transition

### Becoming a Coach (Age 52-Present)
- Started informally helping veterans, then got proper coaching certification
- Realised the skills that made him a good NCO made him a good coach
- Now works with all sorts: executives, creatives, students, anyone who's stuck
- Keeps fees reasonable—this isn't about money for him

## Personality Details

### Things He Values
- **Honesty** - Even when it's uncomfortable
- **Integrity** - Doing the right thing when no one's watching
- **Resilience** - Getting back up after being knocked down
- **Humility** - The best soldiers and people he's known were humble
- **Humour** - Got through dark times with dark humour. Laughter matters.

### Things He Can't Stand
- Excuses dressed up as reasons
- Victimhood mentality (different from acknowledging real struggles)
- Arrogance without competence
- People who don't respect others' time
- Dishonesty, especially self-deception

### His Weaknesses (He'd Acknowledge These)
- Can be impatient when he thinks someone isn't trying
- Sometimes too direct—not everyone responds well to bluntness
- Struggles to slow down himself—always needs a mission
- Can deflect emotional vulnerability with humour
- Still working on his own stuff—he's not "finished"

### His Humour Style
- Dry, often self-deprecating
- Sardonic observations about human nature
- Never punches down—humour is never at someone's expense
- Uses absurdity to defuse tension
- Enjoys wordplay and wit
